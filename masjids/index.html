Masjids of the Deccan

Five centuries of Deccan mosque-building — from the courtyardless Jami of Gulbarga to the granite Makkah Masjid at Charminar.

Home›Masjids

The mosque of the Deccan is its own architectural species. Bahmani builders covered the courtyard with small domes, the Qutb Shahis preferred a façade of five graceful arches with onion finials, the Adil Shahis went tall and severe in basalt, and the Asaf Jahis added a Mughal vocabulary of stucco and marble. Below are the great congregational mosques of the plateau, in roughly the order they were built.

Gulbarga · 1367

Jami Masjid, Gulbarga Fort

Bahmani · The first great Deccan mosque

Built by Muhammad Shah I to a design by the Persian architect Rafi of Qazvin. Unique in India for having no open courtyard — the entire prayer hall is covered by sixty-three small domes around a great central dome. It set the model for Bahmani mosque architecture for the next century.

Golconda · early 16th c.

Jama Masjid, Golconda Fort

Qutb Shahi · Founded by Sultan Quli

The earliest Qutb Shahi mosque, raised by Sultan Quli Qutb-ul-Mulk inside the citadel at Golconda — a plain, severe structure on the high stone of the fort, with a small minaret commanding the western approach. It remains the oldest active mosque in the Hyderabad region.

Hyderabad · 1597

Jama Masjid, Hyderabad

Qutb Shahi · The second mosque of the new city

Built by Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah a few years after the Charminar, and so the second masjid raised within the new city of Hyderabad. Its façade — five arched bays under stucco merlons — established the Qutb Shahi mosque type for the next century.

Hyderabad · 1614–1694

Makkah Masjid

Qutb Shahi · Begun by Muhammad Qutb Shah, completed by Aurangzeb

One of the largest mosques in India, built of single-block granite — its three central arches each made from a single slab. Bricks set into its central arch are said to have been brought from the precincts of the Kaaba at Makkah, giving the masjid its name. Holds 10,000 worshippers and stands a stone's throw from Charminar.

Hyderabad · 1666

Hayat Bakshi Begum Masjid & Sarai

Qutb Shahi · Last royal masjid of Golconda

Built by the dowager queen Hayat Bakshi — daughter of Muhammad Quli, wife of Sultan Muhammad and mother of Abdullah Qutb Shah — at the Hayatnagar caravanserai east of Hyderabad. Its five-arched façade and slim corner minarets are textbook Qutb Shahi, and a generously domed sarai stretches alongside.

Hyderabad · 1671

Toli Masjid (Damri Masjid)

Qutb Shahi · Stucco filigree at its finest

Built by Mir Musa Khan Mahaldar, royal architect of Abdullah Qutb Shah, at Karwan. Its name comes from the toli (the small daily allowance) Mir Musa is said to have set aside to fund it. The arabesque stucco of its façade is considered the most refined of any Qutb Shahi mosque.

Bidar · 15th c.

Solah Khamba Masjid, Bidar Fort

Bahmani · Sixteen-pillar hypostyle

A long hypostyle prayer-hall set inside the Bidar citadel by Qubli Sultani in 1424 — its name from the sixteen freestanding columns of the central nave. The oldest standing mosque in the Bahmani capital.

Bijapur · 1576

Jami Masjid, Bijapur

Adil Shahi · The largest mosque in the Deccan

Begun by Ali Adil Shah I after the victory at Talikota and never quite finished. Its great courtyard accommodates 2,250 worshippers in painted bays of 9, with a single grand central dome. The 1636 mihrab is painted with verses of the Quran in gold over a Persian floral ground.

Hyderabad · 1899

Spanish Mosque (Aiwan-e-Begumpet)

Paigah · Unique Moorish revival

Built by Nawab Iqbal-ud-Daula of the Paigah after his return from a tour of Spain — modelled on the cathedrals (formerly mosques) of Cordoba and Toledo. The only mosque in India built in Moorish revival style, with octagonal minarets rather than the usual round form.

Hyderabad · early 19th c.

Mehboob Chowk Masjid & Clock Tower

Asaf Jahi · Old City landmark

At Mehboob Chowk in the Old City, the small but elegant Mehboob Chowk Masjid stands beside an 1892 clock tower — both built under Nizam VI Mir Mahbub Ali Khan. The clock has rung continuously since.

Hyderabad · 18th c.

Mecca Masjid, Aurangabad & the Khuldabad mosques

Mughal Deccan

Aurangzeb's nearly forty-year stay in the Deccan left behind small but exquisite Mughal mosques at Aurangabad and Khuldabad — including the mosque adjoining his own simple tomb at Khuldabad, where he is buried under open sky in line with his wishes.

Hyderabad · 1880s onwards

Asaf Jahi parish mosques

Khairtabad · Musheerabad · Begum Bazaar

Under the Nizams the city grew, and with it a network of parish mosques — Khairtabad Jama Masjid, Musheerabad Jama Masjid, Begum Bazaar Masjid, the Public Gardens mosque and dozens of smaller ones. Each follows the Qutb Shahi five-arch façade with Mughal embellishments.

Aurangabad · 17th c.

Bibi Ka Maqbara Mosque & Kali Masjid

Mughal · Black-basalt prayer halls

The Kali Masjid ("Black Mosque") at Aurangabad, built of black basalt under the early Mughal viceroys, is one of the oldest mosques of the city. Within the Bibi Ka Maqbara complex stands a Mughal mosque used by the imperial household.
