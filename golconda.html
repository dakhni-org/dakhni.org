I · Aghaz Kakatiya Origins · 12th–14th c.

A shepherd's hill called Mankal

From mud rampart to royal citadel

The granite outcrop the Persians would later call Gol Konda — the round hill — was first walled in mud by the Kakatiya kings of Warangal in the twelfth century. Local tradition records that a shepherd boy on the hill discovered an idol there, which the Kakatiyas raised a small fort around. They named it Mankal, after a nearby village.

In 1364, with the Kakatiyas long fallen, the hill passed to the Bahmani Sultanate, which rebuilt it in stone. For a hundred and fifty years it remained a frontier garrison — a strategic salient watching the eastern Deccan and the route to the Coromandel coast. The Bahmanis used Golconda as a holding-prison for political enemies and as a treasury for the Telangana revenues.

By the late fifteenth century the Bahmani court at Bidar was unravelling. Provincial governors were arming themselves, the Deccani and Afaqi factions were at one another's throats, and the strong frontiers were drifting away from central authority. Golconda was about to acquire its own dynasty.

II · Faseel The Three Concentric Walls

Eleven kilometres of granite

An outer rampart, a middle line and the citadel itself

Golconda is built in three concentric rings. The outer wall — eleven kilometres around — encloses the entire town. The middle wall encloses the palaces and bazaars, and the innermost contains the Bala Hisar ("upper fort"), an audience-pavilion at the summit reached by 380 stone steps. Each wall is studded with semi-circular bastions; eighty-seven survive of an original hundred.

The eight gates — chief among them Fateh Darwaza ("Victory Gate"), Banjara Darwaza, and the Mecca Darwaza — are studded with iron spikes against war elephants. Above the Fateh Darwaza is one of the great peculiarities of medieval Indian engineering: a domed acoustic chamber that picks up the clap of a hand at the threshold and conveys it, audibly, to a sentry post on the Bala Hisar a kilometre uphill.

The whole arrangement was the work of the Bahmanis, expanded by the Qutb Shahis, and never breached by direct assault. Aurangzeb's army in 1687 sat outside it for eight months without forcing a single gate.

III · Bunyaad Sultan Quli & Independence · 1518

A Hamadani makes a kingdom

From Bahmani governor to founder of the Qutb Shahi line

Sultan Quli Qutb-ul-Mulk arrived in the Deccan as a young Turkmen noble of the Qara Qoyunlu, fleeing the Safavid revolution in his native Hamadan. The Bahmani sultan Mahmud Shah took him into service in the 1480s, gave him a command in the eastern provinces, and after the rebellion of Bahadur Gilani in 1495 confirmed him as governor of Telangana. He built his seat at Golconda, replaced its mud walls with granite, and quietly began acting as a sovereign.

In 1518 he stopped paying tribute. The Bahmani court, by then a fiction, did not respond. He took the title Qutb Shah ("Pole-star king"), styled himself an independent sultan, and founded what would be, for the next 169 years, one of the wealthiest small kingdoms in Asia.

He ruled until 1543, when, while at prayer in the Jami Masjid at Golconda — at the age of ninety-nine — he was murdered by an assassin sent by his own son Jamshid. The dynasty he founded would outlive that fratricide by seven generations.

Sultan Quli's tomb at the Qutb Shahi necropolis is the oldest of the dynasty's mausolea — austere, unadorned, in the manner of Bidar.

The mines were not at Golconda itself but at Kollur, Paritala and Ramallakota on the Krishna river — "Golconda" was their market and bond, the name engraved on every great stone of the European cabinet rooms.

IV · Almas The Diamond Trade

Every great stone of Europe

Kollur, Tavernier, and the cabinet rooms of the West

For two centuries — roughly 1500 to 1725 — almost every diamond in Europe came from a small cluster of mines on the river Krishna, in territory ruled from Golconda. The mines were worked by an estimated 60,000 men under monsoon and dry season, washing alluvial gravels through wooden sieves; what they recovered was carried by guarded caravan to Golconda and sold there in a single great bazaar.

The French jeweller Jean-Baptiste Tavernier, who visited in 1645 and again in 1666, recorded the trade in detail: he saw the rough Hope diamond changing hands, watched the great Mughal envoys buying for Aurangzeb's father Shah Jahan, and noted that the working stones came in not just to Golconda but also to a satellite market at Ramallakota nearby. His Six Voyages remain the principal Western source.

The trade collapsed within a generation of the Mughal conquest. The mines flooded, the caravans lost their guard, the brilliant of South India was eclipsed in 1725 by the discovery of diamonds in Brazil. Of Golconda's wealth there remain only the stones themselves — and they are now in the Tower of London, the Louvre, the Smithsonian and the imperial vault of Tehran.

V · Sangeen Engineering at the Citadel

A clap at the gate, water at the summit

Acoustics, Persian wheels, and a fortress that watered itself

The acoustic gate is the most famous of Golconda's curiosities. Stand at the threshold of the Fateh Darwaza and clap once: the sound, focused by the dome above and the granite recess in front, is reproduced at the Bala Hisar pavilion a kilometre uphill, audible above the noise of any normal day. It was a security signal — and a proof, every morning, that the chain of bastions was still in working order.

Quite as remarkable, and far less celebrated, is Golconda's water system. The fortress was supplied entirely from inside. A series of Persian wheels, driven by ox-teams on the lower terraces, lifted water from rock-cut cisterns through stepped clay channels to the topmost pavilions. Lead-glazed pipes carried it through the palace courtyards; an internal cooling system used water surfaces to lower summer temperatures by several degrees. It is a system without obvious parallel in Indian fort architecture of its date.

The Mortuary Bath at the foot of the second wall — a domed pavilion where the dead sultans were ritually washed before burial — is a rare survival of the dynasty's domestic engineering. Its plumbing still works.

VI · Hashtaad The Qutb Shahi Line

Eight sultans, one hundred and sixty-nine years

From the founder to the prisoner of Daulatabad

Sultan Quli (r. 1518–1543) founded the line; his son Jamshid (1543–1550), the patricide, ruled briefly and is buried apart from the rest at the necropolis. The infant Subhan Quli reigned for less than a year before being deposed by a council of nobles in favour of his uncle, Ibrahim Quli (1550–1580), under whom Persian poetry flourished and the Telugu peasantry was integrated into the revenue system.

Muhammad Quli (1580–1611), the fifth, founded Hyderabad in 1591 and wrote a divan of fifty thousand verses in Dakhni, Telugu and Persian — the first sovereign in South Asia to do so. His nephew and successor Muhammad Qutb Shah (1611–1626) began the Mecca Masjid; Abdullah Qutb Shah (1626–1672) extended the city eastward and patronised the Telugu poet Kshetrayya at his court.

The eighth and last, Abul Hasan Tana Shah (1672–1687) — a Sufi-leaning prince elevated against the wishes of the conservative faction — appointed the Brahmin brothers Madanna and Akkanna as ministers, raised the kingdom to its diplomatic and revenue peak, and was overthrown by Aurangzeb in 1687. He died a state prisoner at Daulatabad fort in 1699.

VII · Muhasara The Mughal Siege · 1687

Eight months and a postern

How the strongest fortress in the Deccan finally fell

In January 1687 the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb appeared before Golconda with an army variously reckoned at 100,000 to 150,000 men, two hundred siege guns, and an entire imperial bureaucracy in train. He had already absorbed Bijapur the previous year. Tana Shah's offer of tribute was refused; the issue, as Aurangzeb's letters make clear, was not money but the elimination of an independent Shi'a sovereignty in the Deccan.

For thirty-three weeks the fortress held. Mughal sappers tunnelled under the walls; the Qutb Shahi engineers heard them coming and counter-mined. The general Abdur Razzaq Lari, defending in person, took seventy wounds in a single day's fighting and survived. The Telugu archers in the towers could not be dislodged. The monsoon flooded the besiegers' trenches. Plague broke out in the Mughal camp.

The fort was not stormed. On the night of 21 September 1687, an Afghan officer of Tana Shah's household, Sarandaz Khan, was bribed to open a small postern in the eastern wall. A Mughal column poured through. The defence collapsed in hours. Aurangzeb annexed the kingdom into a new subah of Hyderabad, took the Kollur diamond fields into the imperial revenue, and held Tana Shah in the cold dungeon of Daulatabad for the rest of his days. Of the half-billion rupees in Tana Shah's treasury, the Mughal record claims to have removed the entirety — some seven crore ashrafis of gold, the largest single transfer of bullion in seventeenth-century South Asia.

The Royal Necropolis
  • SiteOne kilometre north of Golconda's outer wall, in a single walled garden of 106 acres.
  • TombsTwenty-one in all — seven sultans (Jamshid is buried apart), with consorts, princes and a court physician.
  • ArchitectureSquare chambers, octagonal drums, ribbed onion domes — a transition from austere Bahmani to elaborate Qutb Shahi.
  • DecorationOriginally faced in turquoise and indigo glazed tile; lost to weather, partly recovered under restoration.
  • Restoration2013–2024 — the Aga Khan Trust for Culture led one of the largest single conservation campaigns in the Deccan.

Sultan Quli's tomb (d. 1543) and Muhammad Quli's tomb (d. 1612) bracket the dynasty's architectural development — a single garden in which a Persian-Turkmen tradition was slowly absorbed into a southern, Telugu-Persian-Dakhni one.

VIII · Maqbara The Qutb Shahi Tombs

A garden of twenty-one domes

The most complete dynastic burial-ground in southern India

A short ride from Golconda's outer wall lies the Qutb Shahi necropolis — twenty-one domed tombs in a single walled garden, each commissioned by a reigning sultan for himself, in the manner of a man planting his own cypress. Seven of the eight sultans are buried here; the patricide Jamshid lies apart, in keeping with a court tradition that did not forgive him.

The architecture moves visibly with the dynasty. Sultan Quli's tomb is austere, almost a smaller cousin of the Bahmani style of Bidar. By the time of Muhammad Quli (d. 1612) the domes have grown, the spandrels are picked out in tile, and the inscription bands flow as comfortably in Telugu signature as in Arabic prayer.

Originally faced in glazed tile of turquoise and indigo, the tombs lost most of their colour to weather; the restoration of 2013–2024 returned lime plaster, glaze and lost finials to almost every dome. Today the necropolis is, with the fort itself, the most complete physical record of an Indian sultanate that survives anywhere in the Deccan.
