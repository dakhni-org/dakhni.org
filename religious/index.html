Other Religious Structures

The plural Deccan — its gurdwaras, fire temples, churches, viharas and synagogues.

From the Bahmani period onward, the Deccan was strikingly plural. Its bazaars, ports and courts held Sikhs, Parsis, Christians, Jews, Jains and Buddhists alongside the larger Muslim and Hindu populations — and each community left its own stone, its own sanctuary, its own quiet corner of the plateau. The result is a religious landscape that runs from the 2nd-century BCE Buddhist viharas at Pitalkhora and Ajanta to the 19th-century Atash Adaran of Hyderabad and the marble Takht of Hazur Sahib on the Godavari.

This page brings together the heritage of those smaller but historically important communities — the religious structures of the Deccan that fall outside the masjids, dargahs and Hindu temples (each of which has its own dedicated page on this site). What follows is grouped by community and arranged roughly by historical weight within each.

I · Sikh · Nanded

Takht Sachkhand Sri Hazur Abchal Nagar Sahib

Established 1708 · Nanded, on the Godavari · Sikh

Built at the place where Guru Gobind Singh, the tenth Sikh guru, made his final home and passed away in 1708. One of the five Takhts — the supreme seats of religious authority in Sikhism — its present marble structure was raised in the early 19th century under the patronage of Maharaja Ranjit Singh.

Explore → II · Sikh · Nanded

Gurdwara Mata Sahib Kaur

Within the Nanded gurdwara complex · Sikh

A shrine within the larger Hazur Sahib complex at Nanded, dedicated to Mata Sahib Kaur — regarded in Sikh tradition as the spiritual mother of the Khalsa. The gurdwara forms part of the cluster of historic sites associated with Guru Gobind Singh's last months on the Godavari.

Explore → III · Sikh · Nanded

Gurdwara Banda Ghat

On the Godavari · Nanded · Sikh

A riverside gurdwara on the Godavari, said to mark the place where Banda Singh Bahadur was initiated by Guru Gobind Singh in 1708 before being despatched north to take up the Sikh cause in Punjab. One of the historic sites of the Nanded sangat.

Explore → IV · Sikh · Nanded

Gurdwara Sangat Sahib

Nanded · Sikh

Tradition holds that this was the place where Guru Gobind Singh held his daily sangat — the gathered assembly of his disciples — during the last months of his life at Nanded. It is one of the cluster of memorial gurdwaras that grew up around the Takht in the 18th and 19th centuries.

Explore → V · Sikh · Hyderabad

Gurdwara at Hussaini Alam

Old city · Hyderabad · Sikh

An old-city gurdwara serving the historic Sikh community of Hyderabad — a community whose presence in the Deccan is traced in tradition to the early travels of Guru Nanak, and later reinforced by the Sikh sowars of the Hyderabad Contingent under the Nizams.

Explore → VI · Parsi · Hyderabad

Bai Manekbai N. Chenoy Dadgah

Established 1847 · Bansilalpet, Hyderabad · Parsi

The principal Atash Adaran — sacred fire temple — of the Parsi community of Hyderabad, founded in 1847 after Parsi settlers arrived in the city under Asaf Jahi patronage in the late 18th century. Better known locally as the Bansilalpet Fire Temple, it remains the spiritual centre of the community.

Explore → VII · Parsi · Hyderabad

Parsi Anjuman Aramgah

Hyderabad · Parsi

The dakhma — tower of silence — complex of the Hyderabad Parsi Anjuman, set apart in a walled compound as required by Zoroastrian custom. One of the few such complexes outside the Bombay Presidency, it has served the community since the 19th century.

Explore → VIII · Parsi · Secunderabad

Aramgah & Dadgah, Secunderabad

Secunderabad cantonment · Parsi

A separate Parsi religious complex serving the Secunderabad cantonment community, which grew up around the British cantonment from the mid-19th century. It comprises both a small dadgah (fire temple) and an aramgah for the community's last rites.

Explore → IX · Parsi · Aurangabad

Aurangabad Parsi Dadgah

Aurangabad · Marathwada · Parsi

A small Parsi fire temple serving the historic Parsi community of Marathwada, whose presence in Aurangabad goes back to the 19th-century mercantile expansion of the Bombay-Deccan trade. Modest in scale but kept in continuous service by the local Anjuman.

Explore → X · Christian · Hyderabad

St. George's Church

1864 · Anglican (now CSI) · Hyderabad

The principal Protestant church of British-era Hyderabad, consecrated in 1864 in a Gothic Revival idiom. Originally Anglican, it now belongs to the Church of South India, and remains one of the most prominent ecclesiastical landmarks of the old British Residency quarter.

Explore → XI · Christian · Hyderabad

St. Joseph's Cathedral, Gunfoundry

1869–1875 · Roman Catholic · Hyderabad

A tall, twin-spired neo-Gothic cathedral built between 1869 and 1875 under French Mill Hill missionaries, and the principal Roman Catholic cathedral of the Hyderabad archdiocese. Its whitewashed façade still dominates the Gunfoundry skyline at the heart of the city.

Explore → XII · Christian · Secunderabad

St. Mary's Basilica

1850 · Roman Catholic · Secunderabad

A Catholic basilica of the Secunderabad cantonment, founded in 1850 to serve the British and Anglo-Indian congregations of the garrison town. It was raised to the status of a minor basilica in the year 2000, and remains a centre of Marian devotion in the Deccan.

Explore → XIII · Christian · Medak

Medak Cathedral (CSI)

1924 · Methodist-Wesleyan, now CSI · Medak

Completed in 1924 by the Wesleyan Methodist mission, Medak Cathedral is one of the largest cathedrals in Asia. Built in Gothic Revival in honey-coloured stone, with stained-glass windows commissioned from Edinburgh, it is now the seat of the Medak diocese of the Church of South India.

Explore → XIV · Christian · Secunderabad

All Saints' Church, Trimulgherry

19th century · Anglican cantonment church

A 19th-century Anglican garrison church built to serve the Trimulgherry cantonment of Secunderabad, on the northern edge of the British military lines. A modest but characteristically restrained piece of cantonment Gothic, still in regular use.

Explore → XV · Christian · Aurangabad

St. Francis Xavier's Cathedral

19th century · Catholic · Aurangabad

The 19th-century Roman Catholic cathedral of Aurangabad, and seat of the Diocese of Aurangabad. It anchors the small but long-rooted Catholic community of Marathwada, whose origins in the Deccan trace back through Portuguese and later European missions.

Explore → XVI · Christian · Hyderabad

Wesleyan Methodist Mission Church

Late 19th century · Methodist · Hyderabad

One of the earliest Methodist mission churches of the Deccan, built in the late 19th century as part of the Wesleyan Methodist mission's spread out of the coastal presidencies into the Nizam's dominions. Modest in scale, but historically central to Methodist work in the region.

Explore → XVII · Jain · Yadadri-Bhuvanagiri

Kulpakji (Kolanupaka) Jain Temple

Said to be c. 2,000 years old · Telangana · Shvetambara

A Shvetambara Jain pilgrimage temple in the Yadadri-Bhuvanagiri district of Telangana, traditionally held to be around two thousand years old and one of the principal Jain sites of South India. It houses the Manikya-deva Tirthankara idol, which tradition says has been worshipped since the time of Rama.

Explore → XVIII · Jain · Bidar / N. Karnataka

Bhandasar Jain Temple complex

Early medieval · Bidar region · Digambara

An early medieval Digambara Jain temple complex of the north Karnataka Deccan, set in the broader Bidar region. The complex is part of the larger Jain footprint of the plateau, which preceded and outlived the sultanate period in the smaller mercantile towns.

Explore → XIX · Jain · Hassan

Shravanabelagola

981 CE · Hassan, Karnataka · Digambara

Although in southern Karnataka rather than the heart of the sultanate Deccan, the great Bahubali (Gomateshwara) statue at Shravanabelagola — carved in 981 CE under the Ganga dynasty — is a defining Jain monument of the wider Deccan plateau, and a pilgrimage centre for the Digambara tradition across South India.

Explore → XX · Buddhist · Aurangabad

Ajanta Caves

2nd c. BCE – 6th c. CE · Satavahana & Vakataka

Thirty rock-cut Buddhist viharas and chaityas excavated into a horseshoe of cliffs above the Waghora river, dating from the 2nd century BCE to the 6th century CE under the Satavahanas and the Vakatakas. A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the great monuments of Buddhist art anywhere in the world.

Explore → XXI · Buddhist · Ellora

Buddhist caves at Ellora (Caves 1–12)

6th–7th century · Mahayana & Vajrayana

The first twelve caves of the Ellora complex are Buddhist viharas of the 6th and 7th centuries, in the Mahayana and emerging Vajrayana traditions. They form the earliest stratum of the great Ellora cluster, before the Hindu and Jain excavations of the later centuries.

Explore → XXII · Buddhist · Aurangabad

Pitalkhora Caves

c. 2nd century BCE · Aurangabad district

A cluster of fourteen rock-cut caves in the Satmala range of the western Deccan, among the earliest Buddhist shrines anywhere on the plateau. Dated to roughly the 2nd century BCE, Pitalkhora's Hinayana-period chaityas and viharas predate Ajanta in its earliest phase.

Explore → XXIII · Buddhist · Aurangabad

Aurangabad Caves (Sayyad Hills)

6th–7th century · Buddhist viharas

A small but striking group of Buddhist viharas of the 6th and 7th centuries, cut into the Sayyad Hills overlooking the city of Aurangabad. The caves are remarkable for the refinement of their later sculpture, including some of the finest Tantric Buddhist imagery in the Deccan.

Explore → XXIV · Buddhist · Nalgonda

Nagarjunakonda

3rd–4th c. CE · Ikshvaku · Nalgonda

A great Buddhist university and stupa complex of the Ikshvaku period (3rd–4th centuries CE), once spread along the Krishna valley. The site was relocated to an island in the Nagarjuna Sagar reservoir in the 1950s ahead of the dam's submersion, and is now preserved as an island museum.

Explore → XXV · Buddhist · Andhra

Amaravati Stupa

2nd c. BCE – 3rd c. CE · Satavahana · Andhra

The Mahachaitya at Amaravati, raised by the Satavahanas on the lower Krishna between roughly the 2nd century BCE and the 3rd century CE, was one of the greatest early Buddhist monuments of the Deccan. Much of its sculpted railing — the celebrated "Amaravati Marbles" — now sits in the British Museum and the Government Museum, Chennai.

Explore → XXVI · Jewish · Pune

Bene Israel Synagogue (Ohel David)

1867 · Pune · Bene Israel

The closest historic synagogue to the sultanate Deccan, Ohel David at Pune was built in 1867 to serve the Bene Israel community of the western Deccan. With its tall clock tower and red-brick façade, it remains the principal place of worship for one of the oldest Jewish communities of India.

Explore → XXVII · Bahá'í · Hyderabad

Bahá'í Community Hazira

20th century · Hyderabad · Bahá'í

The Hazira is the principal Bahá'í centre of Hyderabad — established in the 20th century to serve the Bahá'í community that grew up in the former Hyderabad State. It functions as the meeting house and administrative centre for the city's Bahá'ís, completing the spectrum of religious communities that find their place in the Deccan.
