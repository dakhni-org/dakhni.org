I · The Chains

Seven saints, one tree

Every saint recorded in this archive stood inside a chain of masters — and every chain ascends to the same root.

A Sufi's authority is documentary. Each of the seven saints whose lives this archive records held a formal place in a silsila — a chain of teachers in which each master licensed his successor by khilafat, link by link, back through the founder of the order and the generations before him to the Companions of the Prophet ﷺ and to the Prophet ﷺ himself. To ask "who was this saint's master?" is therefore always answerable, and the answers connect: Burhanuddin Gharib and Bandanawaz Gisudaraz meet two links apart in the hospice of Nizamuddin Awliya; Shah Raju Qattal and the Yousufain brothers stand on one Qadiri stem; and all of the chains, followed far enough upward, converge.

The chart below assembles those chains — exactly as each saint's own record on this site documents them — into a single tree, or shajara. The gold cards are the seven saints of this archive; selecting one opens his full record. The filled, coloured cards are the founders of the four classical silsilas of the Deccan — Chishti, Qadiri, Suhrawardi and Naqshbandi — with a fifth, slate-coloured line for the Hamadani family descent of Hussain Shah Wali. Dashed connectors mark links the records document but do not name, or descent by family rather than formal khilafat.

Reading the Tree

This shajara is drawn strictly from the seven saint records of this archive and the Sufism index page; it introduces no names or dates beyond them. A solid line is a master-to-disciple (or master-to-khalifa) relation named in the records — comparable, as the index page notes, to a hadith chain of transmission (isnad). A dashed line marks either links the records document but do not name — as between 'Abd al-Qadir Jilani and Sayyid Yusuf al-Husayni of Bidar, or in the Multan chain above Baba Sharfuddin — or descent by family rather than formal khilafat, as with the Yousufain brothers from Shah Raju Qattal and Hussain Shah Wali from the family of 'Ali al-Hamadhani. Shah Khamosh belonged to the combined Naqshbandi-Qadiri silsila characteristic of early Asaf Jahi Hyderabad; the tree places him on the Naqshbandi stem, which by the classical reckoning ascends through Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, while the other chains ascend through 'Ali ibn Abi Talib. The generations between the Companions and the founders of the orders are shown condensed.
