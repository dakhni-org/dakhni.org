Six Centuries · 1347 — 1948

Dynasties of the Deccan

From Gulbarga to Hyderabad · The Kingdoms That Shaped Dakhni Culture

Six dynasties. Six hundred years. One civilisation born in the heart of India — where Persian poetry met Telugu devotion, Sufi wisdom met warrior courage, and the earliest form of Urdu took shape: the Dakhni dialect.

The First Kingdom

Bahmani Sultanate

1347 – 1527  ·  Capital: Gulbarga, then Bidar

The first independent Muslim sultanate of the Deccan, founded by Alauddin Bahman Shah in Gulbarga after breaking from the Delhi Sultanate. For 180 years, the Bahmanis were the dominant power of the Deccan plateau.

Under Mahmud Gawan's vizierate (1466–81), the sultanate reached its zenith — a polyglot court of Persian scholars, Telugu poets and Sufi mystics that laid the cultural foundation for Dakhni civilisation. Gawan's madrasa at Bidar, with its blue-tiled facade still standing, drew students from Samarkand to Cairo.

The Bahmanis split the realm into four tarafs (provinces) governed by powerful nobles. After Gawan's execution on a forged letter in 1481, those provinces broke away one by one — at Bijapur, Berar, Ahmadnagar, Bidar and Golconda — and the Deccan's age of five sultanates began.

Founders of Hyderabad

Qutb Shahi Dynasty

1518 – 1687  ·  Capital: Golconda, then Hyderabad

Born from the collapse of the Bahmani kingdom, the Qutb Shahis of Golconda became the greatest patrons of Dakhni — the early Deccani form of Urdu — and its literary culture. Sultan Quli Qutb Mulk, a Turkic noble from Hamadan, declared independence at Golconda in 1518 and built the great granite citadel whose acoustic gates still carry a clap to the topmost rampart.

Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah founded the new city of Hyderabad in 1591, laying out the Charminar at its centre to mark the end of plague in his realm. He wrote poetry in Dakhni, Telugu and Persian — the first reigning sovereign in South Asia to leave a literary divan in a vernacular tongue.

The Qutb Shahi diamond trade made Golconda a byword for fabulous wealth from Madrid to Edo: the Koh-i-Noor, the Hope, the Regent and the Daria-i-Noor all passed through their treasury. After a year-long siege, Aurangzeb's Mughal armies took Golconda by treachery in 1687 and the dynasty ended. The Qutb Shahi tombs north of Hyderabad remain South Asia's most complete royal necropolis.

Masters of Metal

Barid Shahi · Bidar Sultanate

1489 – 1619  ·  Capital: Bidar

The Barid Shahis emerged from a Bahmani prime-ministership and ruled from the fortress city of Bidar after the central court collapsed. Though smaller than their neighbours, they produced one of the most distinctive art forms of the Deccan: Bidriware — a blackened zinc-and-copper alloy inlaid with silver and brass, still made by hereditary artisans in the lanes of Bidar after five centuries.

Their court was a nexus of Sufi scholarship under the chain of the Chishti and Qadiri orders, and Bidar remained a centre of Dakhni intellectual life throughout their rule. The shrine of Khwaja Bandanawaz at Gulbarga and the Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan continued to draw seekers from Khorasan and the Arab world.

The Barid Shahis maintained delicate alliances with their stronger neighbours — the Adil Shahis to the west and the Qutb Shahis to the east — for over a century before being absorbed into Bijapur in 1619. The lobed tombs of the Barid sultans on Bidar's eastern plain are among the finest examples of Deccan funerary architecture.

Patrons of Dakhni Poetry

Adil Shahi Dynasty

1490 – 1686  ·  Capital: Bijapur

Yusuf Adil Shah, a Persian-born commander of contested origin, declared independence at Bijapur in 1490 and founded a dynasty that would rule the western Deccan for nearly two centuries. Nine sultans followed across the Sunni–Shia divide, marrying into Maratha and Vijayanagara houses and turning Bijapur into a city of half a million souls.

Ibrahim Adil Shah II (r. 1580–1627) — poet, musician and author of the Kitab-i-Nauras, a treatise on the nine ragas — opened his court to Hindu and Muslim artists alike, signed his decrees with reverence to Saraswati and was buried with his veena. Under him Bijapur became a cosmopolitan capital rivalling any in Asia.

Their architectural masterworks survive intact: the colossal Gol Gumbaz of Muhammad Adil Shah, whose whispering gallery carries a syllable a sevenfold echo across the dome; the lyrical Ibrahim Rauza, called the Taj of the Deccan; and the Malik-e-Maidan cannon, the largest medieval bronze ordnance in the world. Aurangzeb's Mughal armies finally took Bijapur in 1686 after a two-year siege.

The Last & Greatest

Asaf Jahi Nizams

1724 – 1948  ·  Capital: Hyderabad

The Asaf Jahi dynasty was founded in 1724 by Mir Qamar-ud-Din Khan, a Mughal viceroy of the Deccan who took the title Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I and shifted his capital from Aurangabad to Hyderabad. Across seven generations the Nizams ruled the largest princely state in the Indian subcontinent — a realm the size of Italy.

Their saffron flag bore the kulcha, the round flatbread offered by Hazrat Nizamuddin Aulia (or by tradition, by a wandering dervish) at a roadside inn — and with it, a prophecy of seven generations. The dynasty would rule for exactly that span: Asaf Jah I through Mir Osman Ali Khan, the seventh.

The seventh Nizam (r. 1911–1948) ran Hyderabad as a modern state in miniature: his own railway, postal service, currency, broadcasting station and Osmania University — the first Indian university to teach the sciences in an Indian language. TIME magazine put him on its cover in 1937 as the richest man alive, his Jacob diamond used as a paperweight. In September 1948 the Indian Army's Operation Polo ended Hyderabad's separate existence; the state acceded to India after five days.
