Six Centuries · 1347 — 1948

Dynasties of the Deccan

From Gulbarga to Hyderabad · The Kingdoms That Shaped Dakhni Culture

Six dynasties. Six hundred years. One civilisation born in the heart of India — where Persian poetry met Telugu devotion, Sufi wisdom met warrior courage, and a new language emerged: Dakhni.

The First Kingdom

Bahmani Sultanate

1347 – 1527  ·  Capital: Gulbarga, then Bidar

The first independent Muslim sultanate of the Deccan, founded by Alauddin Bahman Shah in Gulbarga after breaking from the Delhi Sultanate. For 180 years, the Bahmanis were the dominant power of the Deccan plateau.

Under Mahmud Gawan's vizierate (1466–81), the sultanate reached its zenith — a polyglot court of Persian scholars, Telugu poets and Sufi mystics that laid the cultural foundation for Dakhni civilisation.

Founders of Hyderabad

Qutb Shahi Dynasty

1518 – 1687  ·  Capital: Golconda, then Hyderabad

Born from the collapse of the Bahmani kingdom, the Qutb Shahis of Golconda became the greatest patrons of Dakhni language and culture. Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah founded Hyderabad in 1591 and wrote poetry in Dakhni, Telugu and Persian.

Their court produced the finest Dakhni literature, and their diamond trade made Golconda synonymous with unimaginable wealth. The famous Koh-i-Noor diamond passed through their treasury.

Masters of Metal

Barid Shahi · Bidar Sultanate

1489 – 1619  ·  Capital: Bidar

The Barid Shahis emerged from Bahmani governors and ruled from the fortress city of Bidar. Though smaller than their neighbours, they produced one of the most distinctive art forms of the Deccan: Bidriware — black alloy inlaid with silver, still made by artisans in Bidar today.

Their court was a nexus of Sufi scholarship, and Bidar remained a centre of Dakhni intellectual life throughout their rule.

Patrons of Dakhni Poetry

Adil Shahi Dynasty

1490 – 1686  ·  Capital: Bijapur

The Adil Shahis of Bijapur were perhaps the greatest royal patrons of Dakhni literature. Ibrahim Adil Shah II — who was himself a poet, musician and scholar — wrote the famous Kitab-i-Nauras, a landmark of Dakhni literature that blended Hindu and Islamic traditions.

They built the magnificent Gol Gumbaz — whose whisper gallery carries sound across its vast dome — as their royal mausoleum. Bijapur under the Adil Shahis was a cosmopolitan capital rivalling any in Asia.

Gol Gumbaz Kitab-i-Nauras Ibrahim II the Poet-King 196 years
Greatest Ruler: Ibrahim Adil Shah II
The Last & Greatest

Asaf Jahi Nizams

1724 – 1948  ·  Capital: Hyderabad

The Asaf Jahi dynasty, founded by Mir Qamar-ud-Din Khan (Nizam-ul-Mulk), was the last and most prosperous of the Deccan dynasties. The seventh and final Nizam, Mir Osman Ali Khan, was once considered the wealthiest man on earth.

Their saffron flag bore the kulcha — a flatbread given by a Sufi saint who prophesied seven generations of rule. The prophecy held true. In 1948, Operation Polo ended the dynasty as Hyderabad acceded to India.
