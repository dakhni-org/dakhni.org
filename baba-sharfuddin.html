HAZRAT BABA SHARFUDDIN · THE SAINT OF THE HILL

The Suhrawardi shaykh of the Bahmani era whose hilltop hospice south of Hyderabad became, over six centuries, the most-loved Sufi shrine of the city — and to a great many Hyderabadis, the saint by whose intercession (with Allah's leave) their daily life is held together.

I · Nasab Lineage and Early Life

A Sayyid traveller from the heart of the Islamic world

The descent of the saint and the traditional accounts of his origin

Hazrat Sayyid Shah Muhammad Sharfuddin Suhrawardi — known in the affectionate Dakhni-Urdu of his own city as Baba Sharfuddin Sahab and Pahadi Wale Baba — was a Sayyid of the Husayni line, descended from the Holy Prophet ﷺ through Imam Husayn ibn 'Ali. The classical accounts of his life preserved by the hereditary custodians (sajjada-nashins) of his dargah record his lineage in a chain of Husayni Sayyids reaching back through Madina and Iraq to the Ahl al-Bayt.

Traditional sources differ on the precise place and date of his birth. The accounts most widely accepted at Pahadi Sharif itself place his origin in the region of the western Iranian plateau or upper Mesopotamia, in the late seventh or early eighth Islamic century (the thirteenth or early fourteenth Christian century). Other accounts speak of an Arabian birth, in or near Madina, with subsequent residence in Iraq for the years of his formal training. What every account agrees upon is that he was, in the medieval Arabic phrase, an 'alim 'amil — a scholar who practised what he taught — and that his education combined the standard Sunni curriculum (Qur'an, hadith, Hanafi fiqh, Arabic and Persian) with the deeper Sufi sciences of the Suhrawardi tradition.

The reverence in which his lineage is held is unusual even by the standards of Sufi hagiography. His title Sayyid Shah is the standard double honorific given to a Sayyid recognised as a saint in the Indo-Persian tradition; the further appellation Baba — "father" — is the Dakhni Urdu term of intimate devotion, reserved in Hyderabad for very few. Across the popular religious life of the city, the simple phrase "Baba Sahab" requires no further qualifier; it is understood to refer to him.

II · Silsila The Suhrawardi Chain · Baghdad → Multan → the Deccan

The chain of teachers

His Sufi initiation traced through Multan to the founder of the order in Baghdad

Baba Sharfuddin belongs to the Suhrawardiyya, one of the four classical Sunni Sufi silsilas established in South Asia during the medieval centuries. The order takes its name from Shaykh Shihab al-Din 'Umar al-Suhrawardi of Baghdad (d. 632 AH / 1234 CE), nephew of Shaykh Abu al-Najib al-Suhrawardi, and author of the foundational Suhrawardi manual 'Awarif al-Ma'arif — a work studied in the Sunni madrasas of the Indian subcontinent into the twentieth century.

From Baghdad the silsila entered the Indian subcontinent through the great khalifa Shaykh Baha al-Din Zakariya of Multan (d. 1267), and from Multan it spread eastward and southward in the following two centuries. By the fourteenth century the Suhrawardi order had established hospices in Sindh, Punjab, Gujarat and — through the Bahmani-era southward migration of scholars — in the Deccan plateau. Baba Sharfuddin's initiation chain is recorded by the dargah's custodians as passing through several intermediate Suhrawardi masters in Iraq and Multan back to Shihab al-Din 'Umar al-Suhrawardi himself.

The distinctive Suhrawardi method, evident in the practice of Baba Sharfuddin's hospice as it has been preserved at Pahadi Sharif, combines strict adherence to the outer Sharia with structured inner discipline: the prescribed five daily prayers performed at the earliest preferred time; the night vigil (tahajjud); regular fasting beyond the obligatory; the recitation of the standard Suhrawardi awrad (litanies) such as the Hizb al-Bahr of Shadhili currency and the Suhrawardi Wird; and silent dhikr in solitude alternating with vocal dhikr in the company of the disciples. The Suhrawardi shaykh, by classical tradition, is also expected to be active in public life — accepting endowments for the maintenance of the hospice, feeding the poor, and offering counsel to rulers when sought.

III · Aamad The Journey South · 14th century

A long road that ended at a hill

His travels and the settlement at the hill south of the future city of Hyderabad

The traditional accounts describe Baba Sharfuddin as a great traveller in the manner of the medieval Sufi shaykhs of the post-Mongol era. After his formal training he is said to have spent years in the holy cities of Makkah and Madina, performed the Hajj, visited the major shrines of Iraq and Syria, and — in the fashion of his time — undertaken the eastward journey through Khorasan, Sindh and the Punjab, stopping at the established Suhrawardi hospices in each region for periods of further study and contemplation. He arrived in the northern Deccan, the accounts agree, during the high years of the Bahmani sultanate.

The reasons recorded for his southward turn are religious rather than political. The Bahmani Deccan in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries was a country in active need of scholarly Islamic establishment: the sultanate had been formally Islamic only since 1347; the chancery was Persian but the people were Telugu, Marathi and Kannada; the standard institutions of Sunni religious life — mosques, madrasas, qadis, settled Sufi hospices — were being founded in his own generation by figures like Khwaja Bandanawaz at Gulbarga and Burhanuddin Gharib at Khuldabad. Baba Sharfuddin's choice to settle in this region, like theirs, was the choice of a scholar going where his work was most needed.

What is distinctive about his settlement is its location. He chose neither one of the Bahmani capitals (Gulbarga, Bidar) nor an established city, but a low rocky hill in the open countryside south of the Musi river — a place then quite uninhabited, a day's ride from the Qutb Shahi-era city of Hyderabad that would not be founded for another two centuries. The site is now in the Himayath Sagar / Attapur area of greater Hyderabad, on the southern road toward what is now the international airport.

IV · Pahad The Choice of the Hill · the founding of Pahadi Sharif

A khanqah without a city

Why the saint chose an empty hilltop, and what he built there

The hill on which Baba Sharfuddin settled — known ever since as Pahadi Sharif, "the noble hill" — is a low granite outcrop rising perhaps 60 metres above the surrounding plain. In his time the country around it was open agricultural land, with the nearest substantial settlement at the Qutb Shahi-period village of Bagameedi some distance to the north. The saint chose the summit. The classical tradition records that he built there a small mosque and a hospice of compacted earth, drew water from a natural spring on the eastern face of the rock (the spring is still the source of the dargah's baoli, the stepped tank that pilgrims visit today), and lived in the manner of the early Suhrawardi shaykhs of Multan — strict observance, open hospitality, daily teaching of those who climbed the hill.

The deliberate withdrawal from a city was, in his case, both spiritual and practical. The Sufi literature of his era treats the choice of an uninhabited place as a means of khalwat (seclusion) and of training in dependence on Allah for both sustenance and student traffic. In practice it also meant that a saint of his stature, choosing to live in this way, drew disciples to him rather than the reverse: pilgrims, then disciples, then villagers, then merchants settled on the lower slopes; a small settlement grew up at the foot of the hill within his lifetime; and the road from the plain to the summit became the well-worn pilgrim path it remains today. The kitchen at the foot of the hill, which still serves langar daily, traces its institution to his lifetime.

The dargah complex as it now stands — with its hilltop mosque, the saint's tomb under its modern dome, the secondary tombs of his immediate khalifas and family, the baoli, the lower kitchen and pilgrim quarters, and the long covered stairway up the eastern face of the rock — has accreted over six centuries. The hill itself, and the location of the saint's grave at its summit, is what he himself established and is what every subsequent generation has preserved.

V · Ta'lim The Discipline of the Hospice

A method strict in the Sharia, generous in the heart

The practice of his hospice as preserved by the silsila

The method Baba Sharfuddin taught his disciples is the classical Suhrawardi method, modified by the specific conditions of the Bahmani-era Deccan in which he was working. The outer foundation was strict Hanafi fiqh: the five daily prayers in congregation; meticulous observance of the obligatory fasts of Ramadan; the giving of zakat from any property received as endowment; the maintenance of the recommended sunan and supererogatory nawafil. No spiritual exercise was permitted to a disciple who had not first mastered the obligations of the Sharia.

The inner cultivation centred on dhikr in its several Suhrawardi forms: the silent dhikr-i-khafi (the remembrance of Allah in the heart, without movement of the tongue, performed in solitude); the vocal dhikr-i-jali (audible group remembrance, performed at the appointed times in the hospice); the recitation of the Asma' al-Husna (the ninety-nine names); the daily wird of Suhrawardi prescription; and the recitation of salawat upon the Prophet ﷺ. Above these stood the constant practice of muraqaba — disciplined contemplation of Allah's attributes — which the saint considered the heart of his method.

To this internal discipline he added the outer practice characteristic of every successful Sufi hospice: the open kitchen, in which any visitor regardless of station or faith was fed; the open audience, in which any seeker could ask his question; the open burial ground, in which the poor of the surrounding villages could be interred near the saint without distinction. The accounts record that he refused, throughout his life, all offers of state patronage from the Bahmani sultans, and that he accepted from private donors only what was required for the daily maintenance of the kitchen and the hospice. The pattern set by his lifetime governs the dargah administration to this day.

VI · Wisaal His Passing · the founding of the shrine

"Bury me where I lived"

The saint's wisaal and the first generation of the dargah

The classical accounts of Baba Sharfuddin's wisaal (his "union" with Allah — the Sufi term for the death of a saint) place it in the closing decades of the Bahmani period, probably in the late fifteenth century, though the precise year is not fixed by any contemporary chronicle. He had, in the manner of senior Suhrawardi shaykhs, prepared his disciples for his passing; he had appointed his eldest khalifa (successor) from among them; and he had asked, in the simple instruction recorded in the dargah's shajra (the document of succession), to be buried on the summit of the hill where he had lived. The instruction was followed within hours of his passing.

The first marker on his grave was a simple raised earthen platform with a stone slab, in keeping with the modest funerary practice that classical Sunni jurisprudence prefers for the burial of saints. Within a generation his disciples and the local notables who had received teaching from him raised the first formal structure: a low whitewashed mausoleum with a small dome, a screen of jali stone around the grave, a small mosque immediately adjacent, and a covered prayer hall for the use of the visiting pilgrims who were already, by that time, climbing the hill in large numbers. The structure was extended and refurbished under successive Qutb Shahi and Asaf Jahi patrons; the present white-marble dome dates from the mid-nineteenth century.

The first 'urs — the annual commemoration of the saint's wisaal — was observed in his successor's lifetime and has been observed continuously every year since. By the eighteenth century, under the early Asaf Jahis, it had become one of the major pilgrimage events of the Deccan. The dates of the urs have stabilised in the modern era around the early days of the Islamic month of Rabi' al-Thani; the gathering at the dargah on those nights regularly draws several lakh pilgrims.

VII · Dargah Through the Qutb Shahi and Asaf Jahi centuries

A shrine on the road to a city

How successive dynasties tended Pahadi Sharif as the hill became Hyderabad's southern gate

When Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah founded Hyderabad in 1591, the road from the new city southward toward the Krishna passed beneath the hill on which Baba Sharfuddin lay. The dargah, in a single generation, ceased to be a remote khanqah in open country and became — geographically, ritually and commercially — the southern gate of the city's spiritual landscape. The Qutb Shahi sultans tended the shrine through formal endowments (inams) for the maintenance of the lamps, the kitchen and the upkeep of the buildings. Sultan Muhammad Qutb Shah and his successors are recorded in the Qutb Shahi farman archives as personal visitors to the shrine.

Under the Asaf Jahis (from 1724) the patronage was substantially enlarged. The Nizams of Hyderabad held the saint in particular regard; several of them are recorded as visiting the dargah in person, especially before major military campaigns or moments of state difficulty. The fourth Nizam, Nasir-ud-Daulah (r. 1829–1857), endowed the rebuilding of the principal dome in white marble. The sixth Nizam, Mahbub Ali Khan (r. 1869–1911), made substantial additions to the lower pilgrim quarters and the kitchen. The seventh Nizam, Mir Osman Ali Khan, continued the same patronage; the railway link from central Hyderabad to the foot of the hill was developed in his reign, easing the pilgrim journey from the city.

The administration of the shrine has been, throughout these dynastic shifts, hereditary in the family of the saint's successors — the sajjada-nashin, the seated khalifa, who is responsible for the daily affairs of the dargah, the conduct of the urs, the management of the endowments, and the unbroken transmission of the silsila to a new generation of disciples. The present sajjada-nashin is the lineal successor in this chain.

VIII · Aaj Pahadi Sharif Today · 1948 to the present

The most-loved dargah of Hyderabad

A daily institution in the religious life of the modern city

Pahadi Sharif today is one of the most-visited Sufi shrines in the southern Deccan and, by general agreement among Hyderabadis, the most beloved of the city's own dargahs. Several thousand pilgrims climb the hill on an ordinary day; during the annual 'urs and on Thursday and Friday evenings (the traditional Sufi visiting times) the number rises into the tens of thousands. The pilgrim demography spans the entire social field of greater Hyderabad — old-city families and new-city professionals, rich and poor, Sunni and Shia, and not a few Hindu and Christian Hyderabadis who come, in the long Deccan tradition of cross-community devotion to the saints, to seek Baba Sahab's intercession.

The institutional rhythm of the shrine has remained substantially unchanged since the Asaf Jahi period. The five daily prayers are observed in the hilltop mosque under the imam appointed by the sajjada-nashin. The langar serves a continuous daily meal at the foot of the hill. The Thursday evening qawwali, in which the Persian and Dakhni verses of Khusrau, Bandanawaz and Bedil are sung beside Suhrawardi naat and manqabat (poems in praise of the Prophet ﷺ and of the saint), is the most-attended weekly religious gathering of the southern half of the city. The baoli on the eastern face of the rock continues to provide water to the kitchen.

For very many Hyderabadi families, the relation to Baba Sahab is a daily one. Newborns are brought to the dargah for the saint's blessing; the bismillah of children's education is sometimes performed there; couples about to marry pay their respects before the wedding; the sick are brought up the hill; the dead are remembered in the saint's lifetime company. The phrase "Baba Sahab ka karam" — "Baba Sahab's grace" — is one of the most-used expressions of the city's spoken Dakhni. Six centuries on, the hill the saint chose for its emptiness is the spiritual centre of one of the great cities of South Asia.

A Note on Sources

The record above draws on the shajra documents and oral hagiography preserved by the sajjada-nashins of the Pahadi Sharif dargah, the standard Hyderabadi works on the city's Sufi history (notably Sayyid Sirajul Hasan's Tarikh-i-Hyderabad, the Imam-i-Hind series, and the entries in the modern Urdu hagiographical compendia), the gazetteers of the Hyderabad State, and present-day published custodian-family accounts. Several biographical particulars — the precise year of birth, the precise year of wisaal, the route of southern travel — vary between sources; where they do, this record follows the consensus of the Pahadi Sharif tradition itself. The framing of his spiritual practice is straightforwardly that of the classical Suhrawardi manuals ('Awarif al-Ma'arif and the Multan-tradition awrad collections), within the four-madhhab Sunni mainstream.
