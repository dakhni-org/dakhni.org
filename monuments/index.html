Monuments of the Deccan

From the Kakatiya toranas of Warangal to the Asaf Jahi palaces of Hyderabad — the stone record of six centuries.

Home›Monuments

The Deccan school of architecture is a third great Indo-Islamic tradition, distinct from the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughals — its forts are granite and basalt, its mosques small and elegant, its palaces fond of stucco arabesque and onion-dome. Below are the principal monuments of the plateau: Kakatiya temple-toranas, Bahmani citadels, Qutb Shahi tombs and arches, Adil Shahi domes, and the Asaf Jahi palaces of Hyderabad.

Hyderabad · 1591

Charminar

Quli Qutb Shah's four-minaret arch

Built by Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah at the founding of Hyderabad — both a triumphal city-gate and a working mosque on its upper floor. Four 56-metre minarets mark the four cardinal directions, and the building still stands as the symbol of the city.

Hyderabad · pre-1518

Golconda Fort

Qutb Shahi citadel · The diamond capital

A granite citadel rising on a 120-metre hill, with twin concentric walls, eight gates and 87 bastions. The famed clap at the Fateh Darwaza is heard a kilometre away at the durbar — an acoustic alarm built into the design. The fort ruled the diamond trade until Aurangzeb's siege of 1687.

Hyderabad · 16th–17th c.

Qutb Shahi Tombs

South Asia's most complete royal necropolis

In a single walled garden north of Golconda stand twenty-one royal tombs — every Qutb Shahi sultan and many of their queens and ministers, with their stepwells, mosques and mortuary baths. The largest, that of Muhammad Quli, rises in tiered domes 42 metres above the plateau.

Hyderabad · 1860s

Chowmahalla Palace

Asaf Jahi · The seat of the Nizams

Begun by Nizam Salabat Jung and completed by Nizam V, Chowmahalla served as the official residence and durbar of the Nizams of Hyderabad. Its Khilwat Mubarak — the great durbar hall — is lit by nineteen Belgian crystal chandeliers, and its courtyards hold horse-drawn carriages, the Nizam's vintage cars and royal regalia.

Hyderabad · 1894

Falaknuma Palace

Paigah · Italian palazzo on a Deccan hill

Designed in the form of a scorpion by Italian architects for the Paigah noble Vikar-ul-Umra and later sold to Nizam VI, Falaknuma's marble grand staircase, Venetian ceilings and the world's longest dining table (101 seats) made it Hyderabad's most ornate residence. Today it is a Taj heritage hotel.

Hyderabad · 1750s

Purani Haveli

The first Nizam's residence · world's largest wardrobe

The earliest Asaf Jahi palace, built for Mir Nizam Ali Khan and gifted to his son. Its 73-metre wardrobe, walking the entire length of the palace, was used by Nizam VI Mir Mahbub Ali Khan, who is said never to have worn the same outfit twice. Now part houses a museum of Asaf Jahi life.

Hyderabad · 1798

King Kothi Palace

Residence of Nizam VII

The personal home of Mir Osman Ali Khan, the seventh and last Nizam, who preferred its modest scale to Chowmahalla. The white-painted palace at King Kothi crossroads housed the Nizam from 1911 until his death in 1967.

Hyderabad · 1803

British Residency · Koti Women's College

Palladian on the Musi

Built for James Achilles Kirkpatrick, the British Resident at Hyderabad, in a strict Palladian style — its colonnaded façade and grand staircase modelled on Kedleston Hall. The Resident's marriage to Khair-un-Nissa is the subject of William Dalrymple's White Mughals. The building now houses Osmania University College for Women.

Hyderabad · 18th–19th c.

Paigah Tombs

Pisal Banda · A marble-jali necropolis

Twenty-seven tombs of the Paigah nobles — the second family of Hyderabad after the Nizams — set in a walled courtyard with stucco friezes and pierced marble screens of extraordinary delicacy. The Paigah style draws on Mughal, Rajput and Deccan idioms simultaneously.

Bidar · 1429–1619

Bidar Fort

Triple-moated Bahmani citadel

A red-laterite citadel set within three concentric moats cut from solid rock. Its sixteen gates lead to the Rangin Mahal — a Barid-period audience hall painted in pierced mother-of-pearl, lapis and gold — and to the Solah Khamba mosque, one of the oldest in the Deccan.

Bidar · 1472

Mahmud Gawan Madrasa

Bahmani · Turquoise faience of Khorasan

Vizier Mahmud Gawan's three-storey college, modelled on the great madrasas of Samarkand and faced in chequered Persian tile. A 1696 lightning strike destroyed one minaret and the central chamber, but the surviving wing still stands, the most ambitious civic monument the Bahmanis built.

Bijapur · 1656

Gol Gumbaz

Adil Shahi · Whispering dome

The mausoleum of Muhammad Adil Shah. Its 44-metre dome, the second-largest unsupported dome of its time, sits on an octagonal drum without pendentives. A whisper at one corner of the circular gallery is heard with perfect clarity at the opposite corner, seven times over.

Bijapur · 1626

Ibrahim Rauza

Adil Shahi · The Taj of the Deccan

The tomb and mosque of Ibrahim Adil Shah II and his queen Taj Sultana — a paired masterpiece set in a walled garden, with stone tracery so fine the windows appear to be cast iron. It predates the Taj Mahal by twenty years and is said to have influenced Shah Jahan's master-builders.

Bijapur · 17th c.

Asar Mahal & Malik-e-Maidan

Court hall & the world's largest medieval cannon

The Asar Mahal once held two hairs of the Prophet brought from Persia; its painted halls are among the finest of late Adil Shahi work. Nearby, the Malik-e-Maidan — "Lord of the Battlefield" — is a 55-tonne bronze cannon, the largest medieval cast cannon in the world.

Aurangabad · 1660

Bibi Ka Maqbara

Mughal Deccan · Aurangzeb's son's gift to his mother

Built by Prince Azam Shah as a memorial to his mother Dilras Banu Begum — wife of Aurangzeb. A smaller, plaster-faced echo of the Taj Mahal in the Deccan, surrounded by Mughal char-bagh gardens, with the Khuldabad hills behind.

Daulatabad

Devagiri / Daulatabad Fort

Yadava → Tughlaq → Bahmani capital

A conical hill-fort with a 200-metre rock-cut moat, dark spiral passages and a single guarded entry. Muhammad bin Tughlaq famously tried to move his capital here from Delhi in 1327. The fort passed to the Bahmanis and then the Mughals, and is one of the most defensible citadels of South Asia.

Warangal · 13th c.

Warangal Fort & the Kakatiya Toranas

Kakatiya · Pre-sultanate Deccan

The four ceremonial toranas (gateways) of the Kakatiya capital still stand at the centre of a concentric mud-wall fort. They are the emblem of modern Telangana and one of the finest examples of late Kakatiya stonework.

Hanamkonda · 1163

Thousand Pillar Temple

Kakatiya · Star-plan stone temple

Built by Rudradeva of the Kakatiya line, the Rudreshwara Swamy temple at Hanamkonda is a triple-shrine star-plan structure, with delicate carved pillars, a basalt monolithic Nandi and an extensive stepped well — a high point of Kakatiya sculpture.

Palampet · 1213

Ramappa Temple (Kakatiya Rudreshwara)

UNESCO World Heritage Site

Built by general Recharla Rudra of the Kakatiya court, the Ramappa temple at Palampet is named after its sculptor — a rare attribution. Its sandbox foundation, floating brick blocks and dancing madanikas earned it UNESCO inscription in 2021.

Gulbarga · 1367

Gulbarga Fort & Jami Masjid

Bahmani · The first Deccan capital

Within the Bahmani citadel at Gulbarga stands the Jami Masjid of 1367 — unique in India for being entirely covered by a series of small domes, with no open courtyard. Its great central dome and four corner domes set the model for Deccan mosque design.
