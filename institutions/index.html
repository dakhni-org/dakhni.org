Institutions of the Deccan

Six centuries of teaching, healing and book-keeping — from the Bahmani madrasa at Bidar to the Asaf Jahi university at Hyderabad.

Home›Institutions

From the great madrasa of Mahmud Gawan at Bidar to Mir Osman Ali Khan's Osmania University, the Deccan has long been a seat of learning. This page records the institutions — colleges, madrasas, libraries and hospitals — that the sultanates and the Nizams endowed across the plateau, many of which still teach, heal and house books five centuries on.

Bidar · 1472

Madrasa-i-Mahmud Gawan

Bahmani · The Cambridge of the Deccan

Vizier Mahmud Gawan's three-storey college and library at Bidar, faced in Persian turquoise tile and built around a courtyard for a thousand students. Its lecture halls hosted scholars from Khorasan, Najaf and Gujarat, and its library held some 3,000 manuscripts — one of the largest in the Islamic world of its day.

Hyderabad · 1595

Dar-ul-Shifa

Qutb Shahi · A free public hospital

Built by Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah beside the Musi, Dar-ul-Shifa was a Unani teaching hospital that gave free medicine, food and lodging to the city's sick. It paired a working clinic with a school of medicine, an apothecary's stores and quarters for visiting physicians from Persia and Arabia.

Hyderabad · 1876

Jamia Nizamia

The seminary of the Nizams

Founded under Nizam Mir Mahbub Ali Khan VI by Imam Muhammad Anwarullah Farooqui, Jamia Nizamia is one of the oldest Islamic universities in the Subcontinent — granting traditional ijaza in fiqh, hadith and tafsir, and producing fatwas for the Hyderabad State. It still stands at Shibli Gunj and continues to teach in Arabic, Persian and Urdu.

Hyderabad · 1891

Asafia State Library

Now the State Central Library, Afzal Gunj

Founded by Mulla Abdul Qayyum as the Asafia Library and elevated to a State institution by Nizam VI, it holds over half a million volumes including rare Arabic, Persian and Urdu manuscripts, illuminated Qurans and the working notes of the Salar Jung archive. Its red-stone building on the Musi was designed in 1936.

Hyderabad · 1918

Osmania University

Asaf Jahi · Urdu-medium higher learning

Founded by Nizam Mir Osman Ali Khan VII, Osmania was the first Indian university to teach the modern sciences and humanities through Urdu (with English as a parallel medium). Its Arts College, built in 1939 by Belgian architect Ernest Jasper, blends Indo-Saracenic, Mughal and Hindu motifs into one of the great campus monuments of South Asia.

Hyderabad · 1923

Nizam College

Affiliate of Osmania, founded 1887

Started in 1887 as a college of the Madras University and re-organised under Osmania in 1923, Nizam College at Basheer Bagh educated generations of Hyderabadi civil servants, jurists and scientists. Its arched façade and clock tower are landmarks of Abids.

Hyderabad · 1929

Nizam's Institute of Medical Sciences (and Osmania Medical College, 1846)

From Hyderabad Medical School to NIMS

The Hyderabad Medical School of 1846 became Osmania Medical College, today the senior teaching hospital of the city. Its sister institution NIMS, founded in 1989, traces back to the Nizam's Orthopaedic Hospital and runs a renowned super-speciality service across the Deccan.

Hyderabad · 1938

Nizamia Tibbi College

Unani medicine in continuity

Founded under Nizam VII as the state's college of Unani medicine, Nizamia Tibbi College trained hakeems in the Yunani tradition of Avicenna and Razi, with a teaching hospital at Charminar. It survives today as the Government Nizamia Tibbi College, one of the few state-supported Unani institutions in India.

Hyderabad · 1951

Salar Jung Museum & Library

The world's largest one-man collection

Built around the private collection of Nawab Mir Yousuf Ali Khan Salar Jung III — Indo-Mughal miniatures, jade hookahs, Tipu Sultan's wardrobe, the veiled Rebecca, and a working musical clock from Lord Clive's England. Its library holds more than 50,000 books and 10,000 manuscripts.

Hyderabad · 1865

Hyderabad Public School & City College

From Madrasa-i-Aliya to the Public School

The Madrasa-i-Aliya was founded by Salar Jung I in 1865 to educate the sons of the Hyderabad nobility in English, Persian and Urdu. It became City College in 1921, while its boarding wing was reorganised as Hyderabad Public School at Begumpet in 1923 — both still active a century later.

Aurangabad · 1963

Maulana Azad College & the older Madarsas of Aurangabad

Mughal Deccan learning

Aurangabad has held madarsas since the Mughal viceroyalty of the seventeenth century. Today Maulana Azad College (founded 1963) and the older Aurangabad Madarsa-i-Aliya continue that tradition, alongside Dr. B. R. Ambedkar Marathwada University, the Marathwada region's premier seat of learning.

Bidar & Gulbarga

Bahmani-era Khanqahs of the Deccan

Sufi seminaries that taught the dialect

Long before formal colleges, the khanqahs of Bandanawaz at Gulbarga and Burhanuddin at Khuldabad served as seminaries — teaching Quran, hadith, music and the local tongue. Their teaching circles were where early Dakhni was first written down as a vehicle of religious instruction.
