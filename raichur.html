I · Rachakallu The Kakatiya Fortress · 1294 CE

A citadel of stone on the granite ridge

How a Kakatiya general fortified a low hill between two great rivers

The hill on which Raichur stands is a low outcrop of the great granite shield of the southern Deccan — the same rock that gives the country its bone-dry colour and its astonishing endurance. The Kannada name Rāyacūru derives, in one widely held reading, from rāya-ūru, "the king's town"; in another from rāca-kallu, the gemstone-rock of which the fort is built.

The fortress itself was raised in 1294 by Gora Gangaya Reddy, a general of the Kakatiya queen Rudrama Devi and later of her grandson Pratapa Rudra, as the southwestern bastion of the Kakatiya kingdom of Warangal. An inscription in old Kannada on the eastern Sailaganga gate records the foundation; another, on the inner wall, names the masons. The walls run for some five kilometres in two concentric rings, with fourteen bastions on the outer line, dressed in massive blocks of cyclopean masonry without mortar.

Within a generation the Kakatiyas were gone. Malik Kafur's Khalji raid of 1310 reduced Pratapa Rudra to tribute; the second sack of Warangal in 1323 ended the dynasty altogether. For a brief interlude the country fell under the Tughluq province of the Deccan, and after 1336 within the new Vijayanagara empire founded on the south bank of the Tungabhadra by Harihara and Bukka.

II · Bahmani Bahmani Sultanate · 1347–1490

The southernmost Bahmani garrison

When the Doab became the seam between two civilisations

When Alauddin Bahman Shah broke from the Delhi Sultanate in 1347 and was crowned at Gulbarga, the southern frontier of his new kingdom ran along the Krishna river. Raichur, on the south bank of the Krishna and the north bank of the Tungabhadra, was the natural anchor of that line. The first Bahmani sultans took it within a decade, and from then until the sultanate's fall it remained their southernmost garrison — a fortress facing, across the Tungabhadra, the towering capital of Vijayanagara at Hampi.

The Bahmanis added an inner citadel to the Kakatiya walls. A second concentric ring of stone, with the Naurang Darwaza on its east face and the Mecca Darwaza on its west, was built in stages between 1380 and 1430. Persian inscriptions of the reigns of Firoz Shah (1397–1422) and Ahmad Shah I (1422–1436) record the work. The mosques inside — the Jami Masjid of the upper citadel and a smaller domed prayer hall against the southern bastion — are among the earliest Bahmani buildings to survive south of Gulbarga.

For all of the fifteenth century the fort changed hands repeatedly. Vijayanagara took it in 1378, lost it in 1380, took it again in 1418 and held it until 1435. Each siege ended in the same way: a long blockade, a negotiated surrender, a new garrison and a fresh Persian inscription on the gate.

III · Doab The Raichur Doab · 1347–1565

The quarrel between two rivers

Why a tract of black-cotton soil drew three centuries of Deccan war

The Raichur Doab — the wedge of land between the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers as they converge towards their confluence — is some 200 kilometres long and 100 broad. Its black-cotton soil yielded two crops a year of cotton, jowar, and oil-seed; its underground granite gave dressed building-stone; and from at least the Mauryan period onwards, its riverbeds and lateritic outcrops yielded gold. (The mines at Hutti, sixty kilometres east of Raichur, are still worked.)

For two hundred years after 1347 the Doab was the bone over which Bahmani and Vijayanagara fought their longest war. The Persian chronicler Ferishta, writing in 1606, counts no fewer than thirteen pitched battles between 1361 and 1520, almost all of them within sight of the Raichur walls. Sultan Mohammed Shah I's campaign of 1366 — the first great Bahmani thrust south — ended at the Tungabhadra; Devaraya II's counter-campaign in 1424 reached as far north as Mudgal; and Mahmud Gawan, the vizier of Muhammad Shah III, rebuilt the fort yet again in 1469.

What was at stake was never merely the land. To hold Raichur was to hold the gateway. The road from Gulbarga to Hampi ran through its bazaars; the tax-rolls of three sultanates and an empire passed under its gate.

19 May 1520 · The Field of Raichur
  • ProvocationIn 1509 Ismail Adil Shah of Bijapur recaptures Raichur from a weakened Vijayanagara; in 1520 Krishnadevaraya marches to recover it.
  • NumbersThe Rayavachakamu credits Krishnadevaraya with seven hundred thousand foot, thirty-two thousand horse and five hundred elephants. Adil Shah's army is roughly half the size — but better gunned.
  • FirangisBoth sides employ Portuguese gunners under Christovão de Figueiredo; Krishnadevaraya's twenty arquebusiers are the decisive arm of his centre.
  • OutcomeThe Bijapur centre breaks on the third assault. Ismail Adil Shah escapes across the Krishna; his elephant train, gun-park and treasury are taken.
  • AftermathKrishnadevaraya enters Raichur fort, restores the Vijayanagara administration, and grants the gunners a village.

The Portuguese soldier-of-fortune Fernão Nunes, who was at Vijayanagara within seven years of the battle, gives the only surviving eye-witness account of the field — published in his Chronica dos Reis de Bisnaga around 1535.

IV · Yuddha Krishnadevaraya vs Ismail Adil Shah · 19 May 1520

The greatest battle the Deccan ever saw

When gunpowder, cavalry and rival civilisations met on a black-soil plain

The Battle of Raichur is, by every contemporary account, the largest pitched engagement fought in southern India before the modern era. Krishnadevaraya, sixteenth king of the Tuluva line of Vijayanagara, crossed the Tungabhadra in March 1520 at the head of an army that — in the figures of his court poet Peddana — outnumbered the entire population of Bijapur city. Opposing him was Ismail Adil Shah, third sultan of Bijapur, in the seventh year of his reign and already a veteran of Deccan war.

The battle was fought on 19 May 1520 on the open plain south-west of the fort. It lasted, by Nunes's reckoning, "from the third hour until the ninth" — about six hours of continuous fighting. Krishnadevaraya's victory was decisive: the field was held, the fort surrendered without a siege, and the king is said to have entered Raichur on the morning of the 20th to give thanks at the temple of Mallikarjuna inside the citadel.

The triumph did not last. Krishnadevaraya was generous: he returned Bijapur's prisoners and the bulk of its treasury, and offered Ismail Adil Shah a free pardon. Forty-five years later, the same Bijapur, in coalition with the three other Deccan sultanates, would meet Vijayanagara again at Talikota on 26 January 1565 — and the empire that had won so brilliantly at Raichur would be ground to dust.

V · Adil Shahi Bijapur Sultanate · 1565–1686

A second sultanate's citadel

From Talikota to Aurangzeb, the fort under the green flag of Bijapur

Raichur returned to Bijapur in the great division of the Vijayanagara spoils that followed Talikota. For the next 120 years it was an Adil Shahi sarkar, garrisoned by Bijapur troops, surveyed by Bijapur officers, and ornamented by Bijapur architects. The most distinctive monument of the period is the Ek Minar ki Masjid — the One-Minaret Mosque — inside the lower fort, with its slender octagonal tower of black trap and its inscription dating the building to the reign of Ibrahim Adil Shah II.

The fort's gate-houses were re-faced under Mohammed Adil Shah (r. 1626–1656). The Naurang Darwaza received its present iron-studded teak doors and the two carved tigers — the Adil Shahi totem — flanking the inner arch. A short Persian inscription above the lintel records the carver's name (Mir Ahmad of Bijapur) and the year, 1043 AH (1633 CE). The same hand may be seen on the Doddi Darwaza, whose calligraphy is among the finest of the late Adil Shahi school.

Inside the citadel, the Adil Shahis maintained a Sufi establishment around the dargah of Hazrat Sayyid Shah Abdul Hameed Hussaini, a fifteenth-century Qadiriya saint whose urs is still observed. Its annual fair, held in the lunar month of Muharram, drew pilgrims from as far as Bidar and Gulbarga and continues into the present day.

VI · Mughal Mughal Subah · 1686–1724

A brief imperial tenancy

When Raichur fell with Bijapur to the Mughal armies of Aurangzeb

Bijapur fell to Aurangzeb on 12 September 1686 after an eighteen-month siege. Raichur — too distant for serious resistance, too valuable to leave alone — was occupied without a fight in the same campaign. The fort was incorporated into the new Mughal subah of Bijapur, garrisoned by an imperial thanedar, and assessed for revenue in the great Ain-i-Akbari-style survey that Aurangzeb's officers carried out across the southern Deccan in the 1690s.

The Mughal period was not gentle. Maratha raids reached as far as Raichur in 1696 and again in 1707; the campaigns of Aurangzeb's last decade — fought from his moving camp at Brahmapuri on the Bhima — laid the country waste from Bidar to the Tungabhadra. When the emperor died in March 1707, the Doab country was so depopulated that, in the words of one Persian observer, "the very crows had left the field".

For the next sixteen years Raichur passed from one Mughal officer to another as the empire's centre frayed. The Maratha confederacy raided it; the Nizam-ul-Mulk's appointee held it; the Mughal centre at Delhi could no longer name a successor with any confidence that the order would be obeyed. In 1724 Mir Qamar-ud-Din Khan, Asaf Jah I, defeated Mubariz Khan at Shakar Khelda and made the Deccan a hereditary nizamat of his own. Raichur passed quietly into the new state of Hyderabad.

VII · Riyasat Asaf Jahi District · 1724–1948

A district of the Nizam's dominions

From Asaf Jah I to Operation Polo, two and a quarter centuries under the seven-arched standard

Under the Asaf Jahis, Raichur was reorganised as one of the principal districts of the western Hyderabad subah — first under the Berar-and-Mahaur arrangement, then, from 1865, as a separate zila of the Gulbarga division. Its talukas — Raichur, Manvi, Lingsugur, Devadurga, Sindhanur, Koppal, Yelburga — together formed the southern flank of the Nizam's territory, bounded by the Tungabhadra and beyond it the Mysore kingdom and the Madras Presidency.

The district was rich. Cotton from its black-soil plains went up the railway, opened in 1871 between Wadi and Raichur as the first metre-gauge line of the Nizam's State Railway, to the gins of Sholapur and the mills of Bombay. The gold deposits at Hutti, prospected by British and German engineers under Nizam concession in the 1880s, were taken over by the Hyderabad Gold Mines Company in 1902 — a state enterprise that survived the dynasty and is today the Hutti Gold Mines Limited, the only producing gold mine in India.

The last Nizam, Mir Osman Ali Khan, visited Raichur four times during his reign. His engineers raised the high Tungabhadra Dam upstream at Hospet, a joint project with Madras Presidency completed in 1953 — five years after Hyderabad State itself had ceased to exist. With Operation Polo in September 1948, Raichur acceded with the rest of the Nizam's dominions to the Indian Union. In the States Reorganisation of 1956 it passed from Hyderabad State to the new Mysore State, and in 1973 to its successor — Karnataka.
